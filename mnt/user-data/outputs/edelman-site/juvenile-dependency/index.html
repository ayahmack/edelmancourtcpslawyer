Juvenile Dependency in LA County

The Most Serious Court Proceeding Most Parents Never See Coming

Juvenile dependency is not family court. It is not criminal court. It is a specialized legal system governed by California Welfare and Institutions Code Sections 300 through 366.26 — a framework most parents have never heard of until they are suddenly inside it.

In Los Angeles County, these cases are handled exclusively by DCFS and litigated at Edmund D. Edelman Children's Court in Monterey Park. The busiest dependency courthouse in the United States processes thousands of cases annually, with timelines that move quickly and consequences that are permanent — including termination of parental rights.

Parents in the dependency system are entitled to legal representation. But not all representation is equal. An attorney who appears regularly at Edelman Court, knows DCFS practices, and understands the judges and commissioners there will achieve dramatically different outcomes than an attorney who does not.

Court Proceedings

Every Hearing at Edelman Court — Explained

Each hearing in the juvenile dependency process serves a different legal function. Missing any one of them — or appearing without prepared counsel — can set your case back significantly.

⏱️

Detention Hearing

Held within 72 hours of a child's removal. The judge decides whether the child remains detained in foster care or returns home pending the case. This is the most time-sensitive moment in the entire process — having an attorney here matters enormously.

📑

Jurisdiction Hearing

Held 15–30 days after detention. The court determines whether the allegations in the DCFS petition are true under WIC Section 300. This is a full evidentiary proceeding. We challenge social worker reports, present counter-evidence, and cross-examine witnesses.

📋

Disposition Hearing

If jurisdiction is found, the court sets the case plan — including required services, visitation, and the reunification timeline. We advocate for the least restrictive plan that reflects your family's actual circumstances, not DCFS's standard recommendations.

🔄

6-Month Review Hearing

Six months after disposition, the court reviews your progress. Strong compliance and strong advocacy at this hearing can accelerate the reunification timeline. We prepare you thoroughly and present your progress in the most compelling way possible.

📅

12 & 18-Month Hearings

If reunification has not occurred, the court continues to review the case at 12 and 18 months. These hearings are critical — at 18 months, the court may move toward a permanency plan. We fight at every stage to keep reunification on track.

🛡️

.26 Hearing — Termination of Parental Rights

If DCFS moves to terminate parental rights under WIC Section 366.26, we fight aggressively to protect your rights. This is the most serious proceeding in the dependency system and requires experienced, aggressive representation.

The Law

What Is a WIC Section 300 Petition?

Welfare and Institutions Code Section 300 defines the conditions under which a child may be declared a dependent of the court. DCFS files a WIC 300 petition to initiate dependency proceedings, alleging that one or more of the statutory grounds exist.

Common grounds for a WIC 300 petition include: physical abuse, sexual abuse, neglect, emotional abuse, substance abuse in the home, domestic violence, and inability of the parent to provide care. Each ground has a specific legal definition — and each can be challenged.

DCFS petitions are not convictions. They are allegations. Parents have the right to contest every allegation at the jurisdiction hearing, and experienced legal counsel can make a significant difference in whether the petition is sustained, dismissed, or narrowed.

WIC Section 300 — Common Grounds

  • 300(a) Serious physical harm — non-accidental or substantial risk of harm
  • 300(b) Neglect — failure or inability to provide adequate supervision, food, shelter, or medical care
  • 300(c) Serious emotional damage resulting from parent or caretaker conduct
  • 300(d) Sexual abuse by parent, guardian, or household member
  • 300(g) No provision for support — parent incarcerated, hospitalized, or otherwise unable to care for child
  • 300(j) Sibling abuse — abuse of a sibling creates substantial risk for the child

Juvenile Dependency FAQ

  • Juvenile dependency court is a specialized court that handles cases where DCFS alleges that a child has been abused, neglected, or endangered. In LA County, these cases are heard at Edelman Children's Court. The court operates under a different set of rules, standards, and statutes than family court or criminal court — and requires an attorney with specific dependency experience.

  • "CPS" (Child Protective Services) is a general term for government child welfare agencies. In Los Angeles County specifically, this agency is called DCFS — the Department of Children and Family Services. DCFS is the largest child welfare agency in the United States. When people say "CPS" in the LA context, they typically mean DCFS.

  • Yes — and this is exactly what the jurisdiction hearing is for. At the jurisdiction hearing, parents have the right to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine DCFS witnesses including social workers. The burden is on DCFS to prove the allegations by a preponderance of the evidence. An experienced attorney can challenge the petition, present counter-evidence, and argue that the allegations are unsubstantiated or exaggerated.

  • Most cases last six months to two years. The timeline depends significantly on whether parents comply with their case plan, whether allegations are contested, and the complexity of the family circumstances. Cases where parental rights are litigated can last longer. With strong legal representation and full case plan compliance, many families achieve reunification significantly faster than the statutory maximum timelines.

  • A juvenile dependency lawyer represents parents at every stage of the dependency process — from the first social worker interview through all court hearings. This includes advising clients on what to say (and not say) to DCFS, appearing at detention hearings, contesting allegations at jurisdiction, negotiating case plans at disposition, advocating at review hearings, and fighting termination of parental rights when necessary. The attorney's job is to protect your rights and advance your family's interests at every step.
